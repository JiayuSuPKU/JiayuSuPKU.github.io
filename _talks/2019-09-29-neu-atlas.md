Abstract:
======

Neutrophil is the most abundant type of white blood cell that helps resolve infections and inflammation. The classification of neutrophil subpopulations has traditionally relied on their morphology, surface marker expression, or gradient separation, which do not capture the full neutrophil compartment repertoire. 

In this study, we profiled >25,000 mouse neutrophils using single-cell RNA sequencing, providing a unbiased and comprehensive transcriptional landscape of neutrophils in their steady state and during E.coli infection. Contrast to the coarse-grained morphological classification system, single-cell approach allowed us to identify eight neutrophil subpopulations defined by distinct molecular signatures, including a new circulating mature neutrophil population highly expressing interferon-stimulated genes. In addition, considerable heterogeneity exists within the three mature peripheral blood neutrophils, yielding a functional spectrum of circulating neutrophils. Neutrophil heterogeneity and differentiation are driven by both known and uncharacterized transcription factors. Bacterial infection reprograms the genetic architecture of neutrophil population, alters dynamic transition between each subpopulation, and primes neutrophils for augmented functionality without affecting overall heterogeneity. In summary, single-cell transcriptomics enabled the reconstruction of neutrophil differentiation and maturation trajectories and uncovered neutrophil subpopulations, gene pathways, and regulators of neutrophil function and fate decisions. These data establish a reference model and general framework for studying neutrophil-related disease mechanisms, biomarkers, and therapeutic targets at single-cell resolution.
